### Full Vision Pitch - English (Short Version)

For the past fifteen years, technology has been designed to capture our attention. Our phones, apps and notifications constantly compete for focus. They pull us into screens, feeds and alerts. Technology has become incredibly powerful, but in many ways it has also pulled us away from the moments that matter most.

You see this especially in families. The moments that should feel the most connected — the morning before kids leave for school and the evening before they go to bed — have often become the most stressful parts of the day.

For families with ADHD, this is even more intense. I know this because I live it. I have ADHD. My wife has ADHD. And my son has ADHD.

In our house, things don't go wrong because we don't want to cooperate. They go wrong because ADHD brains struggle with time, transitions and starting tasks.

So parents slowly become reminder machines. And in school, the same thing happens. Children with ADHD are corrected throughout the day because executive functions like starting tasks, managing time and switching between activities are harder for them. Not because they don't want to succeed, but because their brains work differently. That constant correction shapes how a child begins to see themselves.

**That's why we're building Guide.**

Guide is a different kind of technology. Not technology that demands attention, but technology that quietly protects it.

The system combines a simple app with a small wearable wristband that uses subtle haptic signals to guide children through their day.

Instead of constant reminders from parents or teachers, the band simply tells the child what's coming next.

*In one minute: get dressed. Four minutes to get dressed. Next: breakfast.*

The family moves through the morning in a calm and predictable rhythm.

At home, Guide supports morning and evening routines. At school, the same band can automatically connect to the classroom schedule. It can signal when an assignment begins, how much time is left, and when it's time to transition. Teachers don't have to constantly redirect attention, and children gain more independence.

What we're building is essentially a new category of technology: **a wearable system for executive function**. A tool that makes structure something you can feel instead of something you constantly have to remember.

ADHD affects hundreds of millions of people worldwide. Families, schools and healthcare systems are all searching for better ways to support daily structure and independence.

But ultimately, Guide is about something bigger.

**Technology doesn't have to compete for our attention. Technology can help give it back.**

I'm building Guide because I want to bring my children to school calmly in the morning… and put them to bed peacefully at night.

And I believe millions of families want the same thing.

### The Rubber Band Distinction (Positioning)

**"Not punishment. Support. Not a correction. A rhythm."**

Some people might think of the rubber band technique (snapping a band on your wrist as negative reinforcement). Guide is the **opposite** and this distinction is critical:

**Rubber Band:**
- Punishment for failure ("I did something wrong → snap → pain")
- Negative reinforcement
- Reactive and self-inflicted
- Teaches through discomfort

**Guide:**
- Support for success ("Something is coming → gentle vibration → preparation")
- Positive guidance
- Proactive and supportive
- Teaches through rhythm

**The framing:**
"Physical feedback that helps instead of hurts. Not punishment for what you did wrong - gentle support for what's coming next."

This distinction works well for:
- Addressing potential misconceptions
- Highlighting positive psychology approach
- Differentiating from behavior modification tools
- Emphasizing the supportive, caring nature of the product

**In one line:** Guide doesn't tell you when you failed. It helps you succeed before the moment even arrives.
