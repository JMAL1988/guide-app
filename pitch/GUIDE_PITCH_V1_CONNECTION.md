## Why This Exists (The Personal Story)

I have ADHD. My wife has ADHD. My son has ADHD.

In our house, the hardest moments aren't school or work. They're the transitions: getting up, getting dressed, eating breakfast, leaving for school, going to bed.

Not because we don't want to cooperate. But because ADHD brains struggle with time, rhythm, and starting.

So I became a reminder machine. Wake up. Get dressed. Hurry up. We're going to be late.

And my son heard constant corrections. At home. At school. All day.

That shapes how a child sees themselves.

I built Guide because I want to bring my kids to school calmly in the morning and put them to bed peacefully at night.

Without yelling. Without stress. Without either of us feeling like failures.

---

## What Guide Actually Is

A simple wristband that uses gentle vibrations to guide kids through their day.

Not screens. Not notifications. Not another thing competing for attention.

Just a quiet rhythm:

*In one minute: get dressed.*  
*Four minutes to get dressed.*  
*Now: breakfast.*

The family moves together. Calm. Predictable. Connected.

---

## What Guide is NOT (Important Distinction)

You might think: "Isn't this like a rubber band people snap on their wrist?"

**No. It's the opposite.**

**Rubber band snap = punishment for failure**
- "I did something I shouldn't have → snap → pain"
- Negative reinforcement
- Self-inflicted
- Reactive
- Teaches through discomfort

**Guide = support for success**
- "Something is coming → gentle vibration → preparation"
- Positive guidance
- Supportive
- Proactive
- Teaches through rhythm

The physical similarity works - people understand wrist-based haptic feedback can change behavior.

But the psychology is completely different:

**Not punishment for what you did wrong. Gentle support for what's coming next.**

**Not a correction. A rhythm.**

**Physical feedback that helps instead of hurts.**

Guide doesn't tell you when you failed. It helps you succeed before the moment even arrives.

## Where Connection is Missing (The Three Pillars)

### 1. Home

Parents don't want to be reminder machines. They want to sit at breakfast with their kids and actually talk.

But ADHD makes time invisible. Tasks feel impossible to start. Transitions are chaos.

So mornings become battles. Evenings become battles. And everyone feels exhausted.

**Guide creates space for connection by handling the structure so parents don't have to.**

---

### 2. School

Teachers don't become teachers to spend their day redirecting attention.

They want to teach. They want to help kids learn. They want to build relationships.

But kids with ADHD get constant corrections:
- Pay attention
- Start your work
- You have 5 minutes left
- Stop that, start this

The child hears: "You're doing it wrong. Again."

**Guide lets teachers teach and kids learn, without constant correction breaking the connection.**

---

### 3. Healthcare

Therapists and coaches see the problem clearly. They know what helps.

But therapy happens in an office. Life happens at home and school.

The gap between "strategies discussed in therapy" and "actually getting dressed in the morning" is enormous.

**Guide bridges that gap. It brings therapeutic support into the moments that actually matter.**
